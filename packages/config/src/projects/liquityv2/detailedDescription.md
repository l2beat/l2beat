Liquity V2 is an immutable, decentralized borrowing protocol that issues BOLD, an onchain overcollateralized stablecoin soft-pegged to the US dollar. A user locks collateral and draws BOLD against it at an annual interest rate they choose themselves, anywhere from 0.5% to 250%. Three collaterals are supported, each as a fully isolated branch with its own market, Stability Pool, and price feed: ETH (held as WETH), Lido wrapped staked ETH (wstETH), and Rocket Pool ETH (rETH). A borrowing position is called a trove and is represented by a transferable NFT, so ownership of the NFT is ownership of the position.

The core contracts that govern borrowing, liquidations, and redemptions are immutable: there is no admin key, no pause, and no upgrade path over user funds. The only governed component routes a share of protocol revenue to community incentives and cannot touch user funds or change any protocol parameter. Interest paid by borrowers is the protocol's only revenue: on each branch, three quarters of it is paid to that branch's Stability Pool depositors as yield, and the remaining quarter funds initiatives chosen by LQTY stakers through that governed contract.

BOLD's dollar peg is defended without active governance. A hard price floor comes from redemptions: when BOLD trades below a dollar, anyone can redeem it for a dollar's worth of collateral at the oracle price, which is profitable to arbitrageurs and burns BOLD until the price recovers. The ceiling is softer and market-driven, working through the interest rates that borrowers set for themselves. When BOLD is above a dollar, redemption risk is low, so borrowers tend to lower their rates, which makes borrowing and minting fresh BOLD more attractive while lowering Stability Pool yields, so supply rises and demand eases until the price settles back toward a dollar. Below a dollar the same forces run in reverse, as borrowers raise rates to cut their redemption risk, lifting Stability Pool yields and demand for BOLD.

### Opening a trove

To open a trove on a branch, a borrower deposits collateral and draws at least 2,000 BOLD, choosing an annual interest rate between 0.5% and 250%. The BOLD is minted directly to the borrower. The position must be overcollateralized above the branch's Minimum Collateral Ratio (MCR), which is 110% on the ETH branch and 120% on the wstETH and rETH branches. A trove that delegates its rate to an interest-rate batch (see below) is instead held to the MCR plus a Batch Collateral Ratio buffer (BCR) of 10%.

Two costs apply at opening. A fixed gas-compensation deposit of 0.0375 WETH is set aside and later paid to whoever liquidates the trove, or refunded when the trove is closed. And an upfront borrowing fee, equal to about seven days of interest on the drawn amount charged at the branch's average interest rate, is added to the trove's debt. Charging this small upfront amount rather than a large origination fee is what keeps short-term borrowing viable.

Ownership is held as the trove's NFT, so transferring the NFT hands over the whole position. The owner can also delegate specific actions: an add manager may add collateral or repay debt, a remove manager may withdraw collateral or draw more BOLD to a designated receiver, and an interest-rate delegate may adjust only the rate, and only within an agreed band.

### Voluntary actions on a trove

While a trove stays healthy, its owner or an authorized delegate can freely adjust it: add or withdraw collateral, draw more BOLD or repay it, or do several of these at once, as long as the result stays above the branch's MCR and the debt stays at or above 2,000 BOLD. Closing a trove repays all outstanding debt, returns the collateral, and refunds the 0.0375 WETH gas deposit. Accrued interest can be folded into the recorded debt at any point.

Because interest rates are set by borrowers, a trove's rate can be changed at any time. To stop borrowers from gaming redemptions by rapidly repricing, a rate change made within seven days of the previous one carries the same upfront fee as new borrowing (about seven days of interest), while a change after that cooldown is free.

Instead of managing a rate directly, a borrower can delegate it to an interest-rate batch. Anyone can register as a batch manager, declaring a fixed minimum and maximum rate band and an annual management fee capped at 10%. The manager then sets a single interest rate for every trove in the batch, and can change it no more often than a declared minimum period of at least one hour. Joining or leaving a batch always charges the upfront fee, again to prevent rate-gaming.

### Liquidations

A trove becomes liquidatable the moment its collateral ratio falls below the branch's MCR, which is 110% on the ETH branch and 120% on the wstETH and rETH branches (batched troves are held to the MCR plus the 10% buffer). Liquidation is permissionless: anyone can liquidate a list of underwater troves in a single call.

Liquidations are absorbed first by the branch's Stability Pool. The pool burns BOLD from its deposits equal to the liquidated debt and receives the trove's collateral in return. Because the collateral seized is worth more than the debt cancelled, depositors come out ahead: the amount seized is the debt plus a liquidation penalty of 5%, and any collateral beyond that is returned to the trove owner as a claimable surplus. If the Stability Pool cannot cover the entire debt, the remainder is redistributed across the branch's other active troves, which take on the leftover debt and collateral in proportion to their size. Redistribution carries a higher penalty: 10% on the ETH branch and 20% on the wstETH and rETH branches.

The liquidator is compensated for gas out of the trove: the 0.0375 WETH gas deposit, plus a collateral gas compensation of 0.5% of the trove's collateral, capped at 2 units of the collateral token. Whatever collateral remains after the debt, the penalty, and this compensation is credited to the former owner as a surplus they can later claim.

### Redemptions

Redemptions are how BOLD holders defend the peg from below. Any holder can redeem BOLD for an equal dollar value of collateral at the oracle price, and the redeemed BOLD is burned. When BOLD trades under a dollar this is directly profitable, since a redeemer buys BOLD below a dollar and receives a dollar of collateral, and the burning tightens supply until the peg recovers.

A redemption is spread across the branches in proportion to how unbacked each one is, measured as its total debt minus the BOLD held in its Stability Pool. Branches that are shut down, or whose total collateral ratio has fallen below their Shutdown Collateral Ratio, are skipped. Within a branch, troves are redeemed in ascending order of interest rate, lowest first. This is the heart of Liquity V2's design: the interest rate a borrower chooses is also their redemption risk. The cheapest borrowers are redeemed first, so a borrower can move down the queue simply by paying a higher rate. A redeemed trove loses collateral and an equal dollar amount of debt, so aside from the fee there is no net loss to the borrower, only reduced collateral exposure.

Redemptions carry a fee that protects borrowers from being redeemed too cheaply. It starts at a floor of 0.5% and rises with a base rate that grows in proportion to the fraction of BOLD supply redeemed and then decays continuously with a roughly six-hour half-life, so a wave of redemptions makes further redemptions more expensive before the cost relaxes again. The fee is taken from the collateral the redeemer receives. If a redemption leaves a trove holding less than the 2,000 BOLD minimum, that trove becomes a "zombie": it is pulled out of the ordinary queue and set aside to be redeemed first next time, which keeps tiny troves from clogging the ordering.

### Emergency scenarios

As a branch approaches distress, a softer protection engages before shutdown. No borrowing operation is allowed to push a branch's total collateral ratio below its Critical Collateral Ratio (CCR), which is 150% on the ETH branch and 160% on the wstETH and rETH branches. In effect, as a branch nears that ratio, the actions that would weaken it further, such as drawing more BOLD or withdrawing collateral, become blocked until it recovers.

If a branch's collateral can no longer safely back its BOLD, the branch is shut down. This happens in one of two ways. Anyone can trigger a shutdown once the branch's total collateral ratio falls below its Shutdown Collateral Ratio (SCR), 110% on the ETH branch and 120% on the wstETH and rETH branches. A branch also shuts down automatically if its price feed reports an oracle failure, which the feed defines as the Chainlink oracle reverting, returning a non-positive price, or returning an answer older than its staleness window (24 hours for the ETH/USD feed, 48 hours for the staked-ETH feeds).

Once a branch is shut down, all borrowing-side activity on it stops: no new troves, no drawing or repaying BOLD, no collateral movements, no interest-rate changes, and interest stops accruing. What stays open is an orderly wind-down. Borrowers can still close troves and claim any liquidation surplus, and Stability Pool depositors can still withdraw. Crucially, shutdown opens urgent redemption: any BOLD holder can redeem against a list of troves they choose, in any order rather than by interest rate, and instead of paying a fee they receive a 2% collateral bonus. The bonus rewards holders for pulling collateral out and retiring the branch's debt, so the branch can be unwound cleanly.
